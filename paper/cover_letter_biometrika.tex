\begin{letter}{To the Board of Editors\\\emph{Biometrika}}

\opening{Dear Editors,}

Please find enclosed our manuscript entitled \emph{``Permutations accelerate Approximate Bayesian Computation''}, which we would like to submit for publication in \emph{Biometrika}.

In this paper, we propose a new likelihood-free inference framework, \emph{permutation-ABC (permABC)}, which enhances Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) in hierarchical and high-dimensional settings. We consider a structure commonly encountered in real-world settings, where the data are divided into exchangeable compartments, each associated with local parameters, while sharing global parameters across compartments. The key idea is to exploit this exchangeability structure through permutation-based matching, leading to significant gains in computational efficiency. We demonstrate that permABC preserves the desirable convergence properties of ABC while substantially improving scalability, notably through a Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) implementation.





Building on this idea, we further introduce two complementary sequential strategies, termed \emph{Over Sampling} and \emph{Under Matching}, which respectively increase the number of simulated compartments or reduce the number of required matches for acceptance during early inference stages. These two approaches are, to the best of our knowledge, entirely novel and open promising avenues for future methodological investigations. We show that they improve robustness and efficiency in challenging regimes, including those involving model misspecification or outliers.

Our empirical assessment includes synthetic examples and a real-world application to a hierarchical Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered (SIR) model of the COVID-19 epidemic in France, across 94 administrative departments. These experiments confirm the practical relevance and performance of the proposed approach in a high-dimensional parameter space, which could not be effectively addressed using standard ABC techniques. This real-world application served as the original motivation for developing the permABC framework.


We believe that the methodological and computational contributions of this work make it of strong interest to the readership of \emph{Biometrika}. The entire codebase used in this study is openly available at: \texttt{https://github.com/AntoineLuciano/permABC}.

This manuscript is original, has not been published, and is not currently under review elsewhere. All authors have approved the submission and there are no conflicts of interest to declare.

Thank you very much for considering our work. We look forward to your evaluation.
